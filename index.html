AI Senior Consultant candidate vision · Capitalize Analytics

Make AI credible in the room—and useful in the workflow.

My thesis: the strongest AI consultant does not hand strategy to delivery. The work is one continuous proof chain—from the executive decision, through the build, to adoption, evidence, and a client team that can operate what comes next.

The demo is not the finish line. It is the first credible piece of evidence.

The company moment is not “AI is coming.” It is “AI must now land.”

Capitalize already has an entry path, delivery offers, modern platform depth, and a newly launched Claude practice. The role sits where those assets have to become a repeatable client experience.

ENTRY

Ideation has a defined front door.

Capitalize offers a 90-minute AI Ideation Session designed to identify high-impact, low-risk use cases and leave clients with a prioritized roadmap and proof-of-value plan.

The Senior Consultant must turn curiosity into a decision-quality next step, not a brainstorm backlog.

PROOF

Value proof has a clock.

The AI to ROI offer is structured around moving an approved use case to a working AI model in 45 days with governance and measurable value.

Scoping discipline, technical judgment, and evaluation design become commercial capabilities.

SCALE

The Claude practice raises the delivery bar.

In June 2026, Capitalize announced Select Tier status in the Claude Partner Network Services Track, a dedicated Anthropic practice, and production deployments.

Partner credibility has to translate into governed workflow change and durable client ownership.

GROWTH

Delivery and go-to-market are expanding together.

The current careers board includes AI delivery roles alongside account growth, marketing, and strategic alliance hiring around Snowflake, Databricks, and Fabric.

My inference: the practice needs people who can reduce friction between market promise, technical proof, and delivery handoff.

The AI Leverage BenchA role-specific operating model for full-lifecycle consulting.

Capitalize’s own role design says the quiet part out loud: this person must be useful in an executive room and credible in the build. I would treat that duality as the operating system of the role—not a compromise between two jobs.

01

Expose friction

Start with a business decision, workflow delay, quality failure, or recurring exception—not a model category.

02

Define leverage

Choose the point where AI can materially alter economics, speed, quality, or risk, then name the human authority boundary.

03

Build evidence

Create the narrowest credible proof with representative data, explicit evaluation cases, failure modes, and observable business measures.

04

Engineer the handoff

Scope the production path: integrations, permissions, observability, operating ownership, adoption work, and stop/escalation conditions.

05

Leave capability

Train the client team, transfer decision logic and documentation, and create a review cadence so the solution can improve without permanent dependence.

What I would make measurable.

Targets should be set with the practice leadership after baseline discovery. The categories below are the instrumentation I would want before declaring growth or delivery success.

DimensionQuestions the metric should answerCandidate measures to baseline
CommercialAre ideation and demos producing well-qualified work that delivery can honor?Ideation-to-paid-engagement conversion; expansion path; estimate accuracy; proposal cycle time.
DeliveryAre proofs becoming dependable assets rather than one-off theater?Time to first working evidence; scope change and rework; evaluation pass rate; production handoff quality.

First 90 days: earn context, then compound it.

This is a hypothesis for discovery, not a pre-baked prescription. The sequence is designed to protect client delivery while learning where the practice actually loses time or trust.

Days 1–30
Read the system

  • Shadow ideation, sales demos, scoping, active delivery, and retrospectives.
  • Map the path from lead signal to use-case choice, estimate, build, evaluation, handoff, and expansion.
  • Learn reference architectures, security/governance patterns, partner motions, and escalation paths.
  • Interview practitioners and client-facing leaders about where work is repeatedly re-explained or re-built.
OutputPractice friction register, decision-rights map, and evidence baseline for one priority improvement.

Days 31–60
Co-deliver the proof

  • Co-lead ideation and convert one opportunity into a decision brief with clear value, data, risk, human authority, and proof criteria.
  • Build or pair-build a reusable demo/reference pattern within the sanctioned technical stack.
  • Draft a scoping and production-handoff checklist that connects estimate assumptions to delivery reality.
  • Instrument value and adoption measures at the beginning of the engagement.
OutputA tested engagement canvas, evaluation rubric, and delivery handoff pattern attached to real work.

Days 61–90
Lead and teach

  • Lead a bounded engagement or major workstream end to end with leadership support.
  • Run a retrospective against estimate, proof criteria, client adoption, and reusable practice learning.
  • Turn validated learning into one enablement session, one reusable asset, and one documented stop condition.
  • Propose the next practice improvement only after evidence shows where leverage exists.
OutputClient evidence, practitioner learning, and a prioritized next bet grounded in observed friction.

The strongest objection should be surfaced, not hidden behind adjacent keywords.

Hiring objection memoDirect response
The concern

“Is Russell current and hands-on enough for a consulting role that expects production-ready AI delivery across modern data platforms—not only strategy and operations leadership?”

The truth

I should not claim production ownership of every platform named in the posting. My verified technical toolkit includes Python, SQL, TensorFlow, LLM/RAG patterns, agentic workflows, human-in-the-loop design, and hands-on AI-assisted operating transformation. My strongest advantage is the combination: technical fluency plus direct accountability for the workflows AI must improve.

The bridge

That combination is relevant to a role explicitly designed around executive advisory, ideation, scoping, demos, solution design, and delivery. I bring direct evidence in operating transformation, high-consequence engineering, AI opportunity design, and cross-functional mechanism building.

The proof plan

I would welcome a practical case: frame a use case, identify the decision and human authority boundary, write the proof/evaluation plan, sketch the architecture, and pair-review implementation choices. The right answer to a hands-on concern is work, not adjectives.

Questions I would bring into the executive conversation.

These are designed to reveal how the role actually creates leverage across market promise, delivery reality, and client ownership.

Where does the AI practice currently lose the most momentum between ideation, scope, proof, production handoff, and client adoption?

What outcome matters most for this hire in the first six months: billable delivery, presales conversion, reusable practice IP, partner motion, or a deliberately balanced mix?

How are decision rights divided among AI & Advanced Analytics, Data Engineering, Process Automation, account growth, and strategic alliances when an opportunity crosses practices?

What has Capitalize learned from the first production Claude deployments that should change how ideation sessions are run or engagements are scoped?

Which parts of the 45-day model are intentionally standardized, and where do you want senior consultants to exercise judgment rather than follow a playbook?

When an AI proof is technically successful but behavior does not change, who owns the adoption problem—and how is that learning carried into the next engagement?

What would make the leadership team say after one year, “this person made the whole practice easier to sell, deliver, and trust”?
